About Solid

Solid is an open standard for structuring data, digital identities, and applications on the Web.

Solid aims to support the creation of the Web as Sir Tim Berners-Lee originally envisioned it when he invented the Web at CERN in 1989. Tim sometimes refers to Solid as “the web - take 3" — or Web3.0 — because Solid integrates a new layer of standards into the Web we already have. The goal of Solid is for people to have more agency over their data.

Solid

Solid is a specification that lets people store their data securely in decentralized data stores called Pods. Pods are like secure personal web servers for your data.

People control access to the data in their Pod. People decide what data to share and with whom (be it individuals, organizations, and/or applications). You can revoke access at any time.

To store and access data in your Pod, Solid-enabled applications use standard, open, and interoperable data formats and protocols.

Data

People can store any kind of data in a Solid Pod — from structured data to files that one might store in a Google Drive or Dropbox folder.

What makes Solid special is the ability to store data in a way that promotes interoperability. Specifically, Solid supports storing Linked Data. Structuring data as Linked Data means that different applications can work with the same data.

Fully interoperable standards

All data in a Solid Pod is stored and accessed using standard, open, and interoperable data formats and protocols. Solid uses a common, shared way of describing things and their relationships to one another that different applications can understand. This gives Solid the unique ability to allow different applications to work with the same data.

Access

With Solid's Authentication and Authorization systems, one can determine which people and applications can access your data. People can grant or revoke access to any slice of their data as needed. Consequently, people can do more with their data, because the applications they decide to use can be granted access to a wider and more diverse set of information.

And just as someone can share their data with others, other people can also share their data in return. This creates rich and collaborative experiences across a combination of both personal and shared data.

Solid Servers and Pods

A Solid Server hosts one or more Solid Pods. Pods are where you store your data:

Each Pod is fully controlled by the Pod owner (i.e., you). And each Pod's data and access rules are fully distinct from those of other Pods.

An entity can get a Pod from a Pod Provider, or they may choose to self-host their Pod.

Entities can even have multiple Pods. They can be hosted by the same Pod Provider or by different Providers or be self-hosted or any combination thereof. The number of Pods an entity has, as well as which Solid Server or Servers they use, is effectively invisible to the applications and services that they use. This is because, in the Solid ecosystem, data is linked through one's Identity and not through the specifics of their Pod. This is true for one's own data as well as for data that others have shared with them.
